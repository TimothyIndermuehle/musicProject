Not too long-winded episode of Music That Matters this week, but instead a setlist that revolves around U2's 1997 ninth studio album Pop. Pop has a techno or EDM groove and was much less organic than Boy or The Unforgettable Fire. Relying heavily on MIDI sampling and timing software, Pop songs are something one could dance to, and some of the songs sound like what I would imagine lounge music to sound like if I were in a cool club in Berlin or Amsterdam. This setlist has a theme but is also one of my texture setlists and is meant not to be in your face sound experience like some of my snowboard lists. This setlist should be good for snowboarding bumps or trees next year when I get a pass again at Loveland Ski Area

U2 has sold a ton of records since I first heard them on WMSE Milwaukee when I was in high school in Kewaskum. When bands like Black Flag and Suicidal Tendencies were screaming angst and screaming for social change on college radio, U2 found a voice to scream the same message with more harmony and pure unadulterated beauty. Named after the application form for unemployment benefits in Britain called a U2, the band and I have supported Amnesty International since the eighties. Lead singer Bono is truly my hero, and I have had a guitar man-crush on guitarist The Edge dating back to my first U2 vinyl purchase October in 1983.

If you were to ask me one reason I moved to Colorado while going to college in Menomonie, the first, I would say is the video for Sunday, Bloody, Sunday, filmed at Redrocks Music Theater in the foothills west of Denver. In 1983, while living in the UW Stout dorms, this video played on MTV 1 to 2 times per hour, twenty-four hours a day, seven days a week. It took U2 from a college radio band to the musical monster they still are today with no looking back.

The weather in Morrison before when the video was to be filmed went from poor to almost sleeting to a torrential downpour, letting up only right before the show. When the rain finally cleared it left Redrocks in a swirling mass of Colorado clouds that acted as a great backdrop for the lighting and performance. Morrison, a cool town, sits at a little higher elevation than Denver in Front Range foothills. Colorado topography creates micro-weather events, and that area traps storms. While the weather is typically beautiful at Redrocks, but when it rains there, it POURS. Colorado weather is unpredictable and wild and will change before you notice sometimes. This video is so visual with the clouds, darkness, and red sandstone towers. Redrocks is the land of the dinosaurs because the Morrison formations on the east side of the park date to the late Jurassic period 163 to 145 million years ago. The red rocks of Red Rocks, a set of coarse-grained conglomerate and sandstone beds, date back 290-296 million years ago. The rock, created from the ancestral Rockies that were high in hematite are found in the entire west from the red country of Texas and Oklahoma to the red layers of the Colorado Plateau in Utah & Arizona. It's hard to go anywhere in the four corners region and see some variation of this rock formation

one day in time

Saturday June 6, 2015

The Pepsi Center
Denver, Colorado

The morning of June sixth I started my day at 10:00am working at Coors Field when the Colorado Rockies baseball team hosted the New York Mets with 43,000 people in attendance. We had the June ever-present delay of game threat from lightning, rowdy New York fans, and a skeleton crew on my team because Aramark had events at all of its venues. The warehouse screwed up my orders, alcohol sales were through the roof, and people ate a lot so it was a long day. After closing my alcohol stands at 4:00 it was over to the Pepsi Center for a double shift where we were hosting U2 on their Innocence and Experience tour.

Once at the Pepsi Center I set up all of my alcohol vendor stands because the bartenders were missing, either stuck in traffic or smoking cigarettes claiming to be stuck in traffic. After enduring countless hours of rain delays at Coors Field the previous week I remember walking around the arena thinking "if it rains on me right now we have a bigger problem, lol." Five minutes before doors I finally had a crew, everything was set and I could finally relax a little bit. Once again, it was super crowded and super busy with a sold-out crowd of 18,462 U2 fans. The best part of event management and production is the hurry up and wait aspect of the job. You work your butt off for a little while and then the event unfolds allowing for viewing time of the show and/or the fans. After we cut alcohol sales and service off for the building at the 3/4 mark of the show, I watched them perform Bullet the Blue Sky and Pride (In the Name of Love).

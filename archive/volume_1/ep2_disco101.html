queen of disco

Donna Summer

1948 to 2012
  • Years performing – 1958 to 2010
  • Rock Hall of Fame Inductee– 2013
  • Won 5 Grammy Awards
  • 42 singles in Billboard Top 100
  • Sold over 100,000,000 records
  • Top 40 hit every year - 1975 to 1984

I thought I’d start the new year out with something fabulous and shiny and bright, Donna Summer and the Village People. Grab your boogie shoes and let's get this new year started right. Donna Summer was unquestionably considered the “Queen of Disco” and the “complete package” because she was not a pretty thing wrapped in over-production or marketing. Donna Summer wrote and produced her own music, performed that music, and steered a 52-year career that included acting. The 1970s and the disco era were a time filled with vices and abundant drug use giving less grounded performers countless opportunities to ruin their lives or careers. Donna Summer was NOT one of those people, performing until 2009, three years before she died.

Donna Summer's music was sultry and richly layered in strings, guitars, horns, and rich orchestral arrangements. Sometimes it was stripped down with simple instrumental arrangements but robust vocals. The music was thick and almost velvety in texture, and like most great dance music, was really sexy.

While the Village People's grammy count did not come even close to Donna Summer's, their impact would last for generations and be untouchable. Before the disco era, there were no musical artists that were out even though there were performers who flirted with bisexuality. The Village People were a cop, a construction worker, a leather daddy, a soldier, an American Native chief, and cowboy, all icons of masculinity. The back-up band was a full orchestra and the performances had non-stop dancing. They sang about gay havens such as New York City and San Fransisco. I remember buying the EP for San Francisco / Everyone is a star in LA and having a crush on the blond guy on the extreme right side of the album cover above. The Village People gave us a voice, and gave drunk straight people music to dance in sports arenas and stadiums.

The music and the titles of the music were campy with songs like Macho Man, In the Navy, and Sodom Gomorrah. There was a very homo-erotic movie that came called Can't Stop the Music that featured Kaitlyn Jenner when she was still identifying as Bruce Jenner. history of disco

Roots

The word disco has several meanings depending on who you talk to, but a DISCO is nothing more than a club that plays music that is typically spun from records, or digitally in your music era. The disco era spanned from around 1974 to 1981, and while shortlived it shaped American culture and music for decades to come. Every small town had some sort of disco in bowling alleys and hotel lobbies. Disco music was influenced heavily by the sixties soul and funk artists. The first disco music I remember hearing was from Sly and the Family Stone and Curtis Mayfield on a variety show called Soul Train, featuring soul, funk, and R&B artists. I loved the dancing and the way everybody dressed!

Prior to the Stonewall Riots in 1969, there were no safe places for gay people to hang and dance like straight people could. After the gay community began to gain freedom, discos became those safe places we needed where its own culture was able to flourish. Disco music became the background music for these safe havens because dance music is ALWAYS sexy!

the golden era

Studio 54

April 1977 - January 1980

Things were bumpin' in clubs all over America in the late seventies as if there could be no tomorrow. Every small town had some sort of disco in bowling alleys and hotel lobbies. I can remember getting down in phy-ed class doing the hustle and other fun disco dances in seventh grade. Disco had its way of dressing in bell-bottom slacks and flowing dresses that looked great under dance lights. These clubs ALL had light-up dance-floors like the movie.

The mother of clubs was in New York city called Studio 54. People went to Studio 54 to be seen and was a favorite hangout of celebrities such as David Bowie, Mick Jaeger, and artist Andy Warhol. Studio 54 ended up closing because it's owner was not paying the IRS it's taxes. The movie 54 was entertaining and was a great snapshot of time for the disco era.

One thing about the disco era was there were more professional musicians employed than at any other time in music history because the bands were full orchestras. Disco bands often had full orchestral string sections along with wah peddle guitars and a driving bass drum back-beat. The wah pedal reigned supreme as did funky rhythm guitar parts.

In 1979 a disco record burning event in Comiskey Park where the Chicago White Sox play baseball in when promooters failed to control the gates. It was intended to promote the rock-and-roll lifestyle.

As disco's popularity waned, it was replaced by synthesized New Wave muisc as a form of dance music. While there were few artists that were out during the disco era and rock era, that was going to change in the new wave and goth era . Disco artists such as the Village people helped open doors for future gay dance and club musicians such as the Pet Shop Boys, Frankie Goes to Hollywood, and the Bronski Beat.

one day in time

Donna Summer vs. the Singing Nun

Want to have some fun? Look up in Google the Billboard top song on the week you were born.

After I looked mine up I was shocked by its story because I had no idea about the rabbit hole I found. The Beetles were about to explode in several months in 1964, so the modern rock as we know it hadn't been born yet. I knew the Billboard #1 song was going to be old school but I was expecting Pat Boone (YUCK!) or a girl band. I had no idea it was going to the story of the first rock star!

The Billboard Magazine number one song in America the week of December 31, 1963, was a song called Dominique by The Singing Nun. Vince was born on July 28, 1979, and the Billboard number one song was Bad Girl by Donna Summer. A bit of a contrast at the first encounter. At first, I snickered and made fun of the Singing Nun. "How uncool is this song?" I said to myself. Well, the song isn't that captivating to me but it was a top-selling record in 11 countries for three weeks in December of 1963. The singing nun's name was Jeannine Deckers and was a real nun who left the church because of differences of opinion about contraception and the role of LGBTQ people in the Catholic church. She was a tormented soul because she was a lesbian in the Catholic Church. She had a partner but they both found an early death via addiction and suicide. Her story inspired me despite how sad it is. She was the FIRST gay rock star! I feel bad because I snickered at her.
